Recap and introduction

Right now, here's the scenario:

A visitor gets to your website, reads your book jacket, or sees something else you've written. Most of us are under the impression we're writing to try and convince, persuade, motivate and sell to whoever happens to have their eyes on our paper. But I want to tear that out of your mind, because remember:

We want nothing to do with folks who have no idea what they want.

Rather, we want to talk to the people who DO know what they want, (i.e. what we're selling), and who are highly motivated to buy already - but just need help deciding FROM WHOM.

We talked about this before, but it's worth repeating so you go into this lesson with your head in the right place.

Your job is about helping people do what they already want to do.

If you're writing a fantasy fiction book, you aren't going to write a teaser or summary that panders to people who are looking for a cookbook—and try to convince them why this fantasy fiction story is (obviously) so much better than lasagna recipes. That would be ridiculous, right? Rather, you're going to write a teaser that taps into the thought processes and the desires of those who are already looking for a red hot fantasy fiction read, but need to choose one of the thousands in front of them on the page at Amazon.

If you're an interior designer, you aren't going to write a homepage that panders to people who aren't really sure if they need or want interior design---and try to convince them to let you come make love to their bathroom. Rather, you're going to write a homepage that taps into the thought processes and desires of those who are already looking for an interior designer, but need to choose one of the twenty they're scouting out in their area. (And therefore, you need to position yourself as the perfect fit for the right person.)

Writing compelling copy isn't about compelling the world to love your industry.

Nor is it about compelling your industry to love you. It's about compelling the people who already see you…to choose you.

Now, sometimes you're going to run across a situation where you'll think: But nobody even knows about what I do, so they can't possibly be looking for it. And that does happen. (Though you'd be surprised - people are finding you, even if you think they aren't. Google is magical!)

But take a music therapist, for example. Not a lot of people know enough about music therapy to even think to look for it. So then what?

The key is not in trying to sell music therapy; the key is to sell the outcome of music therapy, and then position the music therapy as the solution. Because there are people already looking for the outcome of music therapy. They just, perhaps, don't know that music therapy is an option to get there.

(And by the way, this is a great example of a time when you should be looking outside of your industry to market yourself. I'd make a beeline for the mommy bloggers, for example, who have an audience full of hundreds of thousands of moms, a decent percentage of which are likely looking for solutions to their kids' disruptive behavior—and music therapy might be that solution.)

Similarly, let's say you're someone who sells canvas prints online. The goal is not to sell canvas prints; it's to sell the outcome of buying a canvas print, and position the canvas prints themselves as the way to that outcome.

So for example, instead of making a statement like:

Our canvas prints are the highest quality in the Tri-State area.

You might say something like:

If a picture says a thousand words, one of our canvases says a thousand "I love yous".

The reason I would write something like that is because the target market isn't buying a canvas print of their three kids; they're buying a statement about themselves.

That's the outcome: A statement that they care about their family. That they're good mothers. That they love their kids. And they're using the canvas print as a medium for expressing that to the world, and most importantly, to themselves.

They're looking to reinforce to themselves that they care about their family, that they're good mothers, and that they love their kids. We're using a statement to reflect back to them their own identity, and reinforce the fact that, yes, by buying this product, you will be a good mom.

The product isn't a product; it's love on canvas.
