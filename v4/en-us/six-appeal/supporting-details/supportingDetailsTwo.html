Organizing our messaging into a framework

So we've gone through creating our emotionally drenched Six Appeal statements. Now, what?

You took your ideal customer's thoughts and turned them into Six Appeal statements. Now, we want to organize these statements into an intelligent skeleton of whatever you're writing, in order to create a psychological roller-coaster that continually creates an emotional high for the reader. Just when they think they're coming down, you bring them right back up, and the final outcome is going to be one big, giant emotional punch in the gut.

And if you remember, that's the entire goal of The Six Appeal Process: Using words to engineer emotion, because people buy on emotion.

More emotional punch = More sales.

So how do we create this skeleton?

How do we take these statements we've created, and organize them into a useful formula that we can actually work with, and use to guide our writing?

Let's take our hypothetical canvas printing business and continue to workshop it together. In fact, since it was a good line earlier, let's name the business Love on Canvas.

The emotional appeal of the name itself generates interest, don't you think? Compare that with a name like, “Detroit Canvas Print Shop.” You see why I love what I do? How could you not love this game? :)

So first thing's first:

Go ahead and open up a blank document.

Second:

I want you to take your original hook statement and write it down. We worked very hard on that, and we want your hook to take center stage. If you've had to go back and edit your hook based on what you've learned in the last few lessons, edit away! The hook is designed to do just that: Hook. (Line & sinker comes next.)

For example, let's say our original hook statement for our canvas print shop was, If a picture says a thousand words, a canvas says a thousand, 'I love yous.'

Third:

After your hook statement, lay out your other Six Appeal statements on the page, as if each one were going to be a separate paragraph. The order doesn't matter yet—just get 'em on the page.

So if we're workshopping Love on Canvas, let's say my target customer is a busy mother who loves her family but barely has time to comb her hair, let alone sit around making scrapbooks and hanging out on Pinterest. And so I went through the Six Appeal statement exercise and this is what I came up with:

The tangible level what they get:

A picture printed on a canvas.

The benefits level what they get:

Original artwork, memories front-and-center, a cool tribute to a loved one, a gorgeous wall they love to look at.

The Six Appeal level what they get:

Thought: I feel like a joke of a human because I'm not a Pinterest, craft-loving mom.
Statement: Good thing you don't have to be a Pinterest goddess to seem like one.
Thought: I don't have the cash for a big, fancy painting---but I need a big fancy something to fill up this wall.
Statement: The best artwork doesn't have to cost like artwork.
Thought: If I see one more thing to say, "Keep Calm and Carry On..."
Statement: Rule #1: Never tell a woman to just "Keep Calm and Carry On."
Thought: I don't want my kids to think I'm a bad mother because I didn't put together 800 scrapbooks of their childhood.
Statement: Scrapbooks put your love in the attic. We put your love on the wall.
Thought: I have no idea what to get anyone for their birthday, let alone something that makes me look charming, thoughtful, loving and creative.
Statement: Put charm, thoughtfulness and creativity on autopilot.
Thought: Picasso was a creepy asshole.
Statement: Good news! You can (finally) stop pretending to like Picasso.
Thought: Screw you, Mrs. Jones. I might not have a huge reindeer display on the roof at Christmas, and I might forget everybody's birthday, but at least I've got a big, giant canvas printed picture of my kids hung smack dab in the dining room. TOP THAT.
Statement: Make the Jone's keep up with you.

So from here, I'll just take the statements themselves, and lay those out on the page.

If a picture says a thousand words, a canvas says a thousand, "I love yous."

Good thing you don't have to be a Pinterest goddess to seem like one.

The best artwork doesn't have to cost like artwork.

Rule #1: Never tell a woman to just "Keep Calm and Carry On."

Scrapbooks put your love in the attic. We put your love on the wall.

Put charm, thoughtfulness and creative on autopilot.

Good news! You can (finally) stop pretending to like Picasso.

Make the Jone's keep up with you.

Fourth:

Now we want to organize our messages in a logical, intelligent order that naturally builds off the hook, reinforces that main theme, goes in the order of most hard-hitting to least hard-hitting, (whereby “least important” is defined as “most trivial”), and feels like a conversation should unfold.

So in this case, the hook is, “If a picture says a thousand words, a canvas says a thousand, “I love yous,” and reflects the main theme of canvas = love, right? That's what our target is really buying: The ability to showcase their love in a way that makes them look good.

So I'm going to rearrange the Six Appeal statements in a way that builds off that hook, reinforces that main message in a logical, cohesive fashion, from most hard-hitting to least hard-hitting, considering, once again, the worldview of my reader. (BUT then ending with the message that sums it up quite nicely.)

Since the first sentence and the last sentence of anything are THE most important of any piece, you'll be more judged by the last sentence you write than you will the entire body of what you've written, so we want to take very good care of these elements. It's why I've made you examine and re-examine your hook so many times. And it's why, using this new information, I expect you to go back and re-examine it again. I know this stuff isn't easy, but if you want your words to sell for you, they can't be lazy, and neither can you.

So as the proprietor of our imaginary business, Love On Canvas, here's how I would re-organize my Six Appeal statements in a way that builds off the hook, reinforces the main message of that hook, in the order of most important to least important, and ending with a bang that sums it all up nicely:

If a picture says a thousand words, a canvas a thousand, "I love yous."

Put charm, thoughtfulness and creativity on autopilot.

Good thing you don't have to be a Pinterest goddess to seem like one.

Scrapbooks put your love in the attic. We put your love on the wall.

Rule #1: Never tell a woman to just "Keep Calm and Carry On."

Good news! You can (finally) stop pretending to like Picasso.

Make the Jone's keep up with you.

So for example, I listed, “The best artwork doesn't have to cost like artwork,” towards the end because right now, when tapping into a theme of demonstrating love, cost isn't taking center stage. First we want to pull on emotional heart strings, ultimately leaving the more “rational” points until later.

This is even more important once you know that studies show—guess what?—you're going to be lucky if most readers read 20% of whatever you're writing. So you always, always need to front load your best, most hard-hitting emotional messages.

This is why, surprise surprise, I went ahead and listed the statement, “Scrapbooks put your love in the attic. We put your love on the wall” as number three, and not immediately following the hook. Because while it would seem to nicely foray into the love theme, it's not our most hard-hitting emotional message.

And this is where a little subjective judgment comes in handy—and maybe even split testing your copy if you want to take it a step further. While obviously the idea is to know your target as well as you can so you can predict which messages are going to be the most hard-hitting for them, it's not always going to be that easy. You're going to have to make some judgment calls, and you're going to have to tweak as you go.

In the meantime, The Six Appeal Process can give you the framework for developing your messaging and fleshing it out in ways that, using the best of your knowledge, are intelligently designed for emotion.

That said? There's no substitute for actively seeking out that knowledge and confirming your insights.
