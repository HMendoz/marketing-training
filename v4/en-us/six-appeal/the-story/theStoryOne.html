Your target's worldview and what it means for what you're selling

As you can see, as made evident by the Don't Mess With Texas campaign, once again, the biggest piece to consider when making an emotional appeal to your audience is, in fact, the audience themselves. (You get this by now.)

We want to get your audience on board with your message, not by merely making your message interesting, but by making your message a part of their identity.

We talked about this with hooks, and it's my hope that this section, and the sections that follow this lesson, will not only pave the way for you to flesh out some supporting emotional copy, but also go back and edit your hook further because, (I've said it once, and I'll say it again...and then again), the writing process is an editing process. And every step of The Six Appeal Process will force you to go back through your work, re-examine what you've written, and add in any new perspectives along the way.

Writing takes thought. It takes times. It takes second thought. It takes clarity, and more specifically, it takes clarity gained through thought. And it takes understanding that you probably won't get it right the first time around. But that's what they made the delete button for, isn't it? Because delete is supposed to happen.

Editing is supposed to happen. Change is supposed to happen. We're human and, while we're pretty darn good at speaking our own native languages, that isn't to say that we're experts at using that language.

If our goal is to make your message a part of your target market's identity, how do we do that? What does that mean? How do we accomplish it?

Well, it's obvious that the only way we can align our message with our target market's identity is by first understanding their identity. Check. That's what the worldview piece of the puzzle was all about. We can't sell people who we don't understand. Not in real life, and certainly not with the written word.

So here's what I want you to do.

I want you to think about the work you did in the first two lessons. Think about the conclusions you were able to draw. And I want you to take a moment to outline some absolute truths about your target market. Things you know to be true about their worldview as it relates to what you're selling.

We don't need to know about their worldview on everything right now; just how their worldview as a whole affects what you're selling.

And then answer the following, using their worldview to guide your answers.

What does your reader think about:

  1. Your industry?
  2. Their problem in relation to your industry?
  3. What you're specifically sellin them?
