Writing isn't the result

Now it's time to get more into the specifics. The nitty gritty of the actual writing, if you will. How can make sense of things like worldviews, and bring them down to earth in a practical, applicable way?

I can't give you a sentence-by-sentence formula, because formulas don't account for complexities, for uniqueness, and most importantly, for capitalizing on quirks. (Quite possibly your best selling point!) The Six Appeal Process is about taking anything you're writing, and helping you lovingly lace it with pieces of emotionally charged language that gets people excited, gets people hooked, and gets people wanting you.

And that means that it needs to be applicable to anything you're writing as well. And that's the goal. To give you the gorgeous gift of brilliant writing - even when you don't consider yourself a brilliant writer - in order to help you get the things that you want in life.

Is it more sales? More clients? More conversions? More money?

Do you want to use it to get into culinary school, or send a plea letter to Congress, or write your children a meaningful love letter?

Do you want to write the pitch letter that'll forever change your life? Write the blog post of the year? Get featured in Oprah magazine?

Writing a book? Asking people for their vote? Writing the product labels for your brand new wine collection? (I definitely need a wine collection.)

Or maybe you just want whoever reads your diary, long after you're gone, to love every single word. ;)

No matter what you're writing, one thing is always the same: Its a human who's reading it.

And therefore, by studying human nature, and worldview, and how the people you're writing for think about things, you're learning far more about writing than it seems. Writing is not a writing process; it's an editing process, which is really a thinking process. A process in which you and I take these little symbols that we, as humans, all memorized, arrange them into these little strings called words that everybody understands, and then figure out how to optimize those arrangements so our words mean something to the right people.

It's almost like planting a code. When done correctly, the right people will know exactly what you mean.

And that's the key right there: We don't just want people to understand what we're saying. We want what we're saying to mean something to them.

There's a difference between understanding and meaning.When there's an understanding, you've processed something in your breain. But when there's meaning? You've processed it in your heart.

Average writing is writing that other people understand.

Sixy writing is writing that other people feel.

Not everybody feels the same about the same things, though. And that's why the first few lessons were so important—to carefully consider the mindset of the person you're talking to. To understand not just their thoughts, but where those thoughts come from. (Worldview.) Because only then can we delicately arrange words in a way that will, for all intents and purposes, become the code.

And I think this is really where the rubber meets the road. Most people are trying to write in a way that “everybody understands.” They're trying to remove any barriers to communication, and write for a general audience. They're trying to remove the code.

But what they fail to realize is that the code is where meaning is found. The code is what signals to the right people that they've found their tribe. The code is what cues people that it's okay to trust you, that you're one of them, that you know what they want, and that you can deliver.

The code is the key.

And that's precisely what we're here to learn with The Six Appeal Process: How to take generic words that anyone will understand, and turn them into words the right people will feel.

Because just like trying to sell to "anyone" is an ineffective marketing plan, trying to write for "anyone" is, too.

The best writers are the best thinkers.

Writing is a thinking process, where, contrary to popular belief, writing isn't the result.
