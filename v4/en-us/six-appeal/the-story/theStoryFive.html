An emotional toehold

So how do we foster that emotional toehold with words?

How do we use words to tell the right mini-stories that get those mirror neurons firing?

Let's start by going back to our primary goal:

to make your story their story.

So we're tasked with making our story feel like their story. And furthermore, we're tasked with verbalizing their story even if they didn't recognize it as their story. And last but not least, if they did recognize it as their story, they might not have been able to verbalize it. Helping them make these connections is your new #1 job.

Why? well, allow me.

Neuroscience shows that synapses occur inside the brain when we've made a connection among various different things. And furthermore, the more random the components connected, the more synapses occur.

As this fascinating experiment by Google eloquently explains:

“Synapses are the basis of creativity. In other words, synapses firing = creative joy. As kids, that happens all the time because everything is new. Everything is foreign. They aren't constrained by the rules about what “goes together.” Why else was putting the Barbie in the toy car wash more fun than putting the car in the car wash? The web frees us to return to this child-like state where we can adventure through a whole array of different, seemingly unrelated, images and clips – be they old, new, from a world away or own backyard – sparking our all important synapses and helping us come up with new combinations and ideas so easily.”

So, when you can return people to a childlike state and help them see things through a fresh lens they never considered, or see themselves in a way they never considered, or help them see your industry in a way they'd never considered, and/or help them see what you're selling in a way they'd never considered, you spark a whole bunch of exciting brain synapses that will accomplish a few things:

  1. Excite their imagination
  2. Make them pay attention. (Remember when we talked about the brain and how, when it reads something it didn't expect, it pays more attention because to the brain surprise means it didn't know something?)
  3. Associate you and your brand with this gorgeous new fascination.
  4. Project the feeling of freshness, discovery, excitement and intrigue onto you - helping you seem different in a sea of sameness, and helping you get the leg up that you NEED.

In other words, as the experiment by Google will confirm:

"Humans aren't necessarily attracted to the allure of the new, as much as we like to see things we're already familar with in a new way."
