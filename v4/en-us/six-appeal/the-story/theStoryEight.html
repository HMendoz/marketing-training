#1-3 of emotionally-charged writing

Let's return to the Facebook post and talk about some of the things that turned an every day tribute into a piece of emotionally charged writing.

As many of you guessed in the exercise that followed, there are several factors that went into making that Facebook post something that most would consider emotionally compelling. So much so, in fact, that many of the commenters claimed it brought tears to their eyes. When you can create a physical emotional response, you know you've hit the jackpot. And you don't even have to go to the casino, you guys. :)

That said, let's begin by reviewing it once again. Afterward, I'll go over the exact elements that went into creating this effect. Then, I'll guide you through a process for how you can implement these elements into your own writing, so by the end of this class, your words won't be words; they'll be emotional heartstrings. And all that'll be left to do?

Is tug

The post, round two:

Today would have been her 70th birthday. I'm wearing her gold aquamarine ring.

i'm wearing it to remember to be gentle, to slow down, and to offer every stranger who ever sets foot in your house the biggest Italian bread sandwich known to man.

To garden always (or at least try), to worship tomatoes, and to write fancy sick notes for my future children (complete with pressed flowers and calligraphy pens).

To see the best in the cranky gas station attendant, to look them in the eye and see their human, to wear black and white as a religion, to sip water from fancy red brandy glasses, and to treat yourself even when you don't feel like you deserve it. (You do.)

To read more crime mysteries, to use paperbacks as a drug, put whip cream in your coffee, and to indulge in quiet time like other people indulge in happy hour.

To sign your name with gorgeous intention (in particular when there are people in line behind you at the grocery store), to lovingly obsess over the details, to always let people have their moment, and to be classy-even when you're angry.

To pick a husband who will put you on a pedestal, to remember never to take him for granted, to love without judging, and to try not to judge much at all, period. (You never know someone's story.)

And most importantly, to put a wildly beautiful stamp on everything you do, and to live, live, live, not as much as you can afford, but as much as you can feel. Because as long as you feel something, the rest are just details.

Love you, mom. A lot of people tell me that you'd be proud of me. But what they don't know is that everything I've accomplished has nothing to do with me, and everything to do with the way I was raised.

And that being the case?

I'm just as proud of you.

Happy 70th birthday.

Element #1: Look! It's a hook!

Surprise! Turns out I wasn't making you write all of those hooks for nothing, after all. The first sentence of this Facebook post is, believe it or not, a hook disguised as an innocent little sentence. See? You can't escape the hook. Not even if you try and beg and whine and scream and kick and yell.

Today would have been her 70th birthday. I'm wearing her gold aquamarine ring.

What makes it an effective hook?

The curiosity gap.

In other words, I don't give it up on the first date. Why buy the cow when you can get the milk for free, right? That's to say, why keep reading if I already know what this is going to be about?

For example, I could have easily said, “Today would have been my mom's 70th birthday,” but I didn't, because that would have gave away the farm. (Let's see how many Old McDonald metaphors can I stick in here.)

You don't want to give people all the information they need to decide if they want to read more or not, because most often, they'll decide they don't—especially if they think something is about your mother. :) And then you're cutting yourself off at the pass.

If your goal is to get them to read the next sentence, you've got to lure them there. You can't give them all the information they need. It's against the law of human nature. What you really need to do is pretend your hook sentences are the equivalent of Angelina Jolie batting her eyes at your reader right before she disappears around the corner. Who wouldn't follow that?

Contrary to what you learned in school, right? Make a thesis statement! State your argument! Be clear! Don't use ambiguous language!

You know what I have to say about that?

Flush-a-roo! Down the toilet. Don't forget to wash your hands on the way out.

Why? Because they forgot to tell you they were talking about academic writing.

And, last I checked, people do not hang on the edge of their seats waiting around to read your term paper. So unless you're writing your graduate thesis on the sperm whale, you need to exile all of that “common sense” from your brain. It has no place in real writing that real people read.

And if you're wondering if it's really possible that one small substitution from “her” to “my mom's” could render a hook statement less effective? You can stop wondering, because I'm telling you that absolutely it can—and WILL.

No matter what you're writing, I always want you to consider the first sentence of everything as the hook—including the first sentence of every single paragraph—and I want you to deliberately craft it as such, too.

Does it take time? Yes. Does it take thought? A lot. Is it sometimes difficult? Absolutely. Does that mean you should skip it? Never.

Writing is a thinking process, where, contrary to popular belief, writing isn't the result.

Never take human curiosity for granted.

Element #2: It's Hyper Specific.

...to offer every stranger who ever sets foot in your house the biggest Italian bread sandwich known to man.

..and to write fancy sick notes for my future children (complete with pressed flowers and calligraphy pens)

...to sip water from fancy red brandy glasses

...to put whip cream in your coffee

...to sign your name with gorgeous intention (in particular when there are people in line behind you at the grocery store)

Details. Very, very specific details.

And yet, most people, when they sit down to write something, do so with the intention to “make it relevant for everyone.” So they talk in general terms. They try not to alienate anyone. They try to make it applicable to anyone who “might be reading.” But that's precisely the point: You should know who you want reading. Do not fall into this trap. That's why we did all that stuff about worldview. Is this making sense now?

Contrary to instinct, including hyper specific details does not alienate people from the experiencie; rather, it makes people feel more included.

We feel connected through the details.

It's as if you get to spy on a secret part of my life, my thoughts, my feelings, and feel them for yourself.

Certainly I knew that not everyone's mother drank water out of a red brandy glass. But I included the detail because it paints a picture, and makes you feel like you're there. And the more you can make someone feel like they're a part of the experience, the more they'll feel. Remember mirror neurons?

Contrast that statement - “...to sip water from fancy red brandy glasses” - with - “She drank water from her favorite glass.”

Contrast “...to offer every stranger who ever sets foot in your house the biggest Italian bread sandwich known to man” with “She was very generous to anyone who ever stopped by.”

Contrast “...and to write fancy sick notes for my future children (complete with pressed flowers and calligraphy pens) with “She was a thoughtful human being.”

The more specifics you use, the more included people feel. Because while you're telling a specific story, you're really using that story to tell their story.

Highlighting the universal in the specific. It's an art form we'll be learning how to do effectively in the next few lessons! Not to mention - this type of writing is just more interesting to read, isn't it?

And here's the even bigger benefit. Ever heard the writing advice to “show, don't tell?” It's great advice. And this is one way that's accomplished. Through specifics. When you use specific details, you can paint a picture about a person without ever having to come out and directly say it. I could have said, “My mom would have been 70 today. She loved to garden, and every time I see a tomato I think of her. She was so thoughtful, always very generous with anyone who visited. I miss her, and I hope she'd proud.” But I think it's pretty obvious that writing falls flat. And the reason it falls flat is because you're telling me instead of showing me.

Element #3: Unexpected Word Pairings

Gorgeous intention.
Worship tomatoes.
Fancy sick notes.
Livingly obsess.

Oh how I love an unexpected juxtaposition of words. And so does every reader. Why? Because it surprises the brain. (And you know how much we're into that.) The brain sees one word and thinks it knows which ones is coming next, so when you throw that off kilter? It gets attention. Bonus? It feels FRESH. And when your writing feels fresh, you know what happens?

You feel fresh.

Remember how we talked about how people are fascinated by finding a fresh perspective on the familiar?

That's why this works

Always look for a new way to say something old. There's power in that.

Element #4: Reinforce Worldview & Sell Them on Themselves

Now here's where things start getting interesting. Everything you write has to be selected with the utmost care. Every detail you include has to be selected with care. Because we want the details, and what those details mean, to line up with the worldview of the person we're writing for.

I wrote that Facebook post for the TMFproject audience: An audience that appreciates an inspirational message, that loves to DIY, that values human connection (in particular in a world more and more dominated by an online connection), that wants to remember to slow down, who want this life to be a wildly memorable adventure. I hand-selected details about my mom to include that would support their worldview.

I can't stress the importance of this.

Remember before when we talked about trying to reinforce your target market's identity? This is one way that you do that. You line up the details you emphasize with the ones that will resonate with their worldview, and help them reinforce their own decisions they make in life.

Consider the other details I could have included about my mom:

  1. She thought everybody in the United States should learn English (something we held VERY different perspectives about)
  2. She showed up with alcohol on her breath to meet my principal & Andrew McKelvey, the founder of Monster.com who was awarding me a full scholarship to college. The principal called me down after their meeting to express his concern.
  3. She relied on government assistance to survive, never once considering starting her own business.
  4. She was scared to death to drive in the snow, in the rain, and at night.

But instead of including those details, I left them out and painted a picture of her being a gentle homebody, a DIYer, someone who appreciated life and knew something the rest of us didn't.

A member of our inaugural Six Appeal class noted this:

“There's something else about this piece. Can't quite put my finger on it, but almost all the memories you share are the opposite of the normal, expected situations. They're all unconventional approaches to living. "Slow down," in a fast paced, non-stop world. "Be kind to strangers and give them a sandwich" when most people will barely look a stranger in the eye, much less smile at them. "Sign your name with gorgeous intention" when most everyone scribbles an indecipherable scrawl of chicken scratch when they sign their name, because they're in a hurry and no one will ever look at their signature on that credit card receipt anyway. Everything you're calling out to remember about what your mom taught you is unconventional and unexpected. It makes me feel inspired to stand up and just fucking be myself, despite the conventions of those around me.”

Remember: You aren't using words to tell a story; you're using them to paint a picture. Make sure you know which picture you need to be painting. (Hint: It's the one that will help them see THEMSELVES in YOU.)

Element #5: It's Says Something.

Yes I'm talking about my mom, but what am I really saying?

Make sure that no matter what you're talking about, the reader can always tease a universal theme or message from your words. We aren't writing for the hell of it; we're writing to communicate something. Check out the TMF blog and notice that all of the lifestyle related posts all carry a universal message about life - even if they're talking about something as silly as a date I went on.

Another inaugural member of Six Appeal noted:

“Wearing the ring allows you to cruise through this list. This is a how to be a better person list. It is telling you to be kind, to slow down, to enjoy, how to treat others. There is contrast. How many life advice lists like this include a cranky gas attendant? This is love but done so differently. It is rich. Your story doesn't have a beginning, middle, end because it is a list. The items don't have to all connect to each other. The connection is that it all comes from your mom's good sense. You pack so much punch into each sentence that you can cover a lot in a small space, like a Facebook post. Like you tell us, every word counts.”

The reason you want to make sure that your writing carries a message greater than the words on the page is not so you can play Mother Teresa, however.

The real reason is this: Because if they like the message, they'll like you.

It's simple

Have you ever found yourself wanting to buy something you totally didn't need-and maybe never will need-only because you loved the message of the company?

Yup.

What you say really is more important than you sell. And people buy things not because they need them (never because they need them), but because of what buying those things says about them as people.

We all wanto to be seen

Weave a message through your writing that helps feel like you SEE them? And they'll not only be on board, but they'll share it with everyone they know.

Because deep down inside, we all want to make a statement about who we are.

And we use products, services and things to illustrate just that:

Our identity.

Element #6: It ends just like it begins: With a bang.

We already talked (a lot) about the hook, but the beginning isn't the only important piece; the end is just as important.

While the beginning compels people to read more, the end is equally powerful because, believe it or not, it's what people remember most. And it's the end that's going to make or break it for them. It's the end that's going to determine if they think, “Yeah!” or “Meh.”

Think about that time you spent two hours watching a great movie, and then the ending is limp. What a disappointment, right? No matter how great the movie itself was, if it has a bad ending, it makes you feel like the whole movie was a waste. You do not want people to feel that way about something you've written.

If you take nothing else from this class, take this: Spend the majority of your time working on the beginning and the end.

The strongest type of ending is the one that loops back around and reinforces something you said in the beginning, makes a connection no one saw coming, brings an unexpected detail back into play, or otherwise closes the story. If you end well, it satisfies the reader in a way that makes him feel like your writing was good. If you end poorly, no matter how good the rest was, he'll still be feeling like there's something missing about your writing - and by extension, about you.

Note the flip that came at the end of the mom story—that I was proud of her.

Study any good writing and you'll suddenly notice that the end is really what makes it, isn't it?
