Mirror neurons and storytelling

Many people commented back on the Facebook post, saying it made them tear up. Others wrote that it made them want to call their mom. And ALL responded with a feeling. So let's unpack this. What is it about this little Facebook post that has the ability to pack such an emotional punch?

There are a number of reasons this little paragraph was able to hit an emotional high note, but first we're going to start with the biggest reason of all, the foundation of everything:

Story.

I have a love/hate relationship with this word. On one hand, it's overused and misunderstood. You hear people talking about storytelling all the time these days.

"Brand storytelling"
"Authentic storytelling"
"The power of story."

And it kind of makes you roll your eyes a little bit, doesn't it?

But do you know why that is?

Because no one's effectively helping you understand what they mean by that.

Tell me that you'll help me craft my “brand story,” and it sounds like you want to make me a mission statement. Translation: Not on the top of my to-do list if I'm a busy, harried, hustling entrepreneur. Do people even read mission statements anymore?

Yet, that's not what they mean at all, and story IS important. Paramount, in fact. Because telling a story helps you naturally connect the dots & strategically call out your target audience, align with their worldview, speak to their perceptions on your industry and what you're selling because of that worldview, and—here's the kicker— do so in a way that's interesting and engaging for them to read. Because that's number one, right?

You might have an interesting message, but if it isn't interesting and/or engaging to read, it won't get read.

But here's the caveat (and the really, really important piece of the puzzle that most people— even the ones talking about “brand stories”—do not necessarily understand):.

We are not talking about telling stories in the same way that most people think of the word "story".

This is where a lot of the confusion comes in. We aren't talking about writing a little essay with a beginning, middle and end. We aren't talking about telling a little bed time story to your customer. How do you put that shit on your services page, right?

Rather, we're talking about telling a story through every single sentence you write.

Whether it's the sentences that make up that services page, your bio, product descriptions, a love letter to your customers, a book jacket, a book, or something else.

We have one goal, and one goal only:

To make your story their story.

And to do so by selecting just the right words that will make them feel like, “Yes. This is what I'm about.”

...Even if they didn't know it. And especially if they couldn't verbalize it prior. (This is one of your greatest secret weapons, so file that idea away for safe keeping.)

Have you ever heard of mirror neurons?

It's what's working behind the scenes in your brain when you see someone stub their toe on a big, fat rock and you instinctively flinch. Or if you witness a car rear-end another car. (Ouch.) Or even when someone smiles at you and you have the instinct to smile back. (Unless they're your significant other and you're angry - then it's poker face to the death.)

“Before the discovery of mirror neurons, scientists generally believed that our brains used logical thought processes to interpret and predict other people's actions. Now, however, many have come to believe that we understand others not by thinking, but by feeling. For mirror neurons appear to let us "simulate" not just other people's actions, but the intentions and emotions behind those actions.”

Some of the most important research around mirror neurons is how it helps us develop empathy for others. Empathy isn't something we have to think about to produce; it just happens. We feel something based on what we see other people feeling.

And do you know why this will change your writing forever?

Because it turns out that, even when just listening to a speaker tell a story, the same exact areas of both the speaker and the listener's brains light up in an fMRI - even though one person was telling the story, and the rest of the people were merely listening to it. (According to this study by Princeton.)

And even more freakily? Theories of neuroscience show that if your mirror neurons are exposed long enough to another person's ideas over any period of time, they actually adapt and change themselves—and this is how learning and growth occurs. (And also why you pick up the habits of your friends.) Because your brain rewires itself. “Cells that fire together, wire together,” as it were.

The part we're really interested in, however?

Is how we can make this happen via the written word.

Because it does happen via the written word.

It's why just reading about scraping fingernails down a chalkboard makes you cringe.

Pick the right words, and the reader will feel them - just as if they were experiencing them. And it's the feeling that we're after. Because that feeling is called emotion. And as we talked about in the beginning, emotions are felt far sooner than any thought or logical response, and are much more responsible for the decisions that your reader is making.
