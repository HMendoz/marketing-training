Get intimate

Read this:

Every Sunday, we gather together as a family to enjoy each other's company, and do the things we don't have time to do during the week. The nice stuff. The stuff I wish I had more time for. Stuff like yoga, and afternoon drives. I don't know what it is about Sundays, but I always come out of one feeling more refreshed; more loved.

Now read this:

Sundays are for heart openers. For backbends and lingering. For open windows and open minds. For Eggs Benedicts, for barbecues, for sharing playlists and for dancing with your invisible top hat to make your little sister laugh. For a windows-down drive with Dad, for phone calls from Grandma. For home healing and home practice. For cartoons, for gardening, for meals we can share. For meditation, for kneeling and praying, for mending, for dreaming, for clarifying the murkiness of your mind. For rest, for coloring books, for congratulations, and sparkly turquoise nail polish. For love, because always for love.

Did you feel that?

The first paragraph is okay. It's not even bad. We all wish we had more time for yoga and afternoon drives. So I'm halfway there. I'm getting inside the reader's head. They're thinking, “Yeah, I wish I had more time for that, too.” (And then whatever follows that you're selling, they're already primed to buy because they're with your thought process, right? Right.)

But the second paragraph is on a whole other level.

The second paragraph makes you desperately yearn for this. The second paragraph, by the end of it, has you standing up on a chair, pummeling a fist into the air shouting, “Yes! Yes! YES!”...à la Herbal Essences commercial. Or weeping into a pillow because all you ever wanted in life was love, dammit. :)

Do you know what the difference is?

The level of detail.

They say the devil is in the details, but really? The brilliant writing is. And this is similar in theme to what we discussed before about making your hook specific, because in the specific story, we find the universal one.

Do I have to love Eggs Benedict to emotionally connect with that post? No. Have I ever even tried an Eggs Benedict? Not once! But that doesn't matter. Because it's not about the object; it's about the feeling of the object.

I love coming home to Denver. Deep exhale. I'm home. At Mama and Papa's house in Denver. At this table, in this room is where we connect. We've fought, we've cried, we've laughed. We've played chess and finished puzzles. We've opened bottles and letters, artfully arranged cakes and platters of pasta. We've written in journals and opened Christmas presents. We've apologized, we've confessed, we've grown. The home that I grew up in and the home I'm creating will always be full of life, this way. Not perfect, not pristine...But ALIVE. Contrasting and awkwardly shaped and completely BRIGHT in every shade. My parents made this room, which would've been the "formal room," the common area, no TV, no distractions. Just a table, wine cellars and art. If you're going to spend time with the people you love, spend time connecting. This is the calm before the storm. In a minute, there will be 6 loud voices, 6 overlapping conversations, 6 unique stories and 6 sounds of laughter all different tones from one another....All different perspectives, all different desires and dreams, all sitting at this same table. The writing is in the details

The writing is in the details

The writing is in the details.
It's why J. Peterman, my favorite example on earth to use for brilliant writing, is so successful. You know that J. Peterman exists outside of Seinfeld, right? Those of you who have taken The Original TMFproject Copywriting Workshop know I'm obsessed.

Why?

Because no one else in the world can sell a product so well without ever showing an actual picture. Except them.

They sell apparel and unique items found in unique places from around the world. And the only product image they show? Is a measly little sketch. There are no close-ups. No way to see the fabric. No way to really see what you're getting until you get it. And that's part of their allure. The treasure. They even have a banner referencing a treasure hunt right on their homepage. These guys get it.

1. They know exactly who they're talking to.

There is no doubt that you're talking to the kind of guy who wants to feel like a hot shot. You aren't talking to a guy who crawls into his 9-5 and barely whispers a peep before crawling back out at the end of the day. You're talking to a mover, a shaker, a wannabe mogul. And so they crafted this story to speak directly to that character. “This is what Howard Hughes wore the day he bought a restaurant and returned to fire a waiter who had been rude.” In other words, you want to have that kind of power? You buy this jacket. And you're one step closer.

1. The story is so specific.

Most people would shy away from telling such a specific story. They'd think, “Well, I wouldn't want to alienate anyone who isn't 231 pounds and 6'6. Or anyone who doesn't know who Howard Hughes is. (I don't, for the record.) Or anyone who doesn't have a father. But guess what? This is exactly what you need to be doing. In this specific story, we find a universal story---a universal message. And because it's specific, we can imagine it. And because we can imagine it, we can feel it. And on a much deeper level than if they had merely said, “This is a jacket for power players. For big boys. For men who mean business.”

And notice? There's not even a real picture of the damn jacket. THIS is what I mean when I say, “What you say is more important than what you sell.” Precisely. Right. Here.

So here's what I want you to do. Go back to your piece. And find at least one spot where you can insert hyper level detail. The idea here is to take blanket ideas and turn them into concrete stories.

For example:

"You want to be the one in charge."

...becomes...

"You want to walk in and command a room-without doing any commanding. Pencils will drop. Pstures will stiffen. And every single person in the room will be praying to god they didn't forget to put their phone on siletn."

You feel the difference?
