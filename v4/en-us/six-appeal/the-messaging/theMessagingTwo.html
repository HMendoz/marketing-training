What do you want your writing to do?

So you're writing something. Now let's talk about what you want that something to accomplish.

Now, the obvious answer here is: We want a sale. A sale of some sort, whether it's a financial transaction, or even just getting someone to opt-in to your website. When you get someone to take an action, that's a form of a sale. No matter what the action is.

So from the reader's perspective, what's the point of reading what you wrote and taking the action that you want them to? What do they get if they buy into your writing? Into you? Into what you're selling?

There are three levels here:

  1. The tangible level what they get.
  2. The benefits level what they get.
  3. Associate you and your brand with this gorgeous new fascination.
  4. The Six Appeal level what they get.

The tangible level stuff is easy. It's the obvious stuff. They get the cookbook. The photographs. The web design. The writing. The coaching. The time. The advice. The insight. The idea. The entertainment. The experience. The memories. The hand-painted necklace.

For example, I might hire a professional photographer because I want the tangible headshots, yes, but on a benefits level it isn't about the headshots, but what the headshots do for me. The benefits of having the headshots might be about feeling more confident that I'll succeed in business because I look more professional. More successful. More with it. More legitimate. More aesthetically...pleasing.

You've probably heard the whole features versus benefits discussion before; it's a generally accepted principle of copywriting that you should sell people on the benefits of something, rather than the features. You get it. This is nothing new. In other words, you aren't selling headshots; you're selling confidence. That's the difference between a feature and a benefit, and it's the difference between the surface level what they get and the benefits level what they get. Check.

But—and this is a big but—now I want to ask you to take it a step even further.

Because emotional messages don't stop at the benefits—contrary to common practice. That's where most people stop—even copywriters. But lazy thinking leads to lazy writing. Benefits Page 79 of 145 are just the beginning of the conversation, because they aren't strong enough to inspire any real emotion. And, if you'll recall, people buy what they love, not what they need. Or, as commonly cited, “People buy on emotion and justify with logic.” So it's our job to inspire that emotion, come hell or high water.

Back to the car metaphor! Are you excited?

Take a shiny white BMW X6 for example. If you were to buy this car, the surface level what you get is the shiny white BMW itself.

Take it a step further, and the benefits level what you get is prestige. A lot of marketing experts will tell you to sell prestige, not a car.

And certainly there's a layer of truth there, but tell me something: If I tell you that you should buy a BMW because you're going to feel prestigious, does that do anything for you?

Do the words themselves do abything for you?

While the concept itself is valid, we still need to develop better ways to put this into words.

So the average person writing this might stick with the benefits, throwing a bunch of adjectives in the big copper pot. They'll tell you that buying a BMW X6 will make you feel proud. More manly. More womanly. More adventurous. More mysterious. More successful. More sexy

Sure, those things are nice.

But nice doesn't evoke emotion.

These words are flatlining the message. We need to take it a step further and delve into the psychological benefits. This is also, in part, where the rest of the copywriting world falters— they only go so deep. Their benefits are still too close to surface level.

We need to go deeper. We need to go to the third level: The Six Appeal Level

Otherwise known as the psychological level. Again, this is why we spent so much time analyzing our readers' worldviews and perceptions prior to putting a single word on the page. Because we need to know what they think about themselves, in order to understand what they think, period. (And how to perch ourselves nicely in their minds.)

So back to our car-tastic white shiny BMW example.

On a tangible level, what I get is a white BMW. On a benefits level, what I get is feeling more successful. More sexy. More adventurous.

BUT—what I really care about is how owning an X6 will complement my perceptions of myself, and my identity to the outside world—whether I recognize that to be a fact or not.

Because even though plenty of people say they don't care what the world thinks, they do. In fact, it's impossible for them not to. As social creatures, we're hardwired not only to care what the others think, but to seek their approval. To be seen by them. To be understood by them. We actually crave their judgment.

This is where benefits fall flat, because benefits are usually just a pile of lifeless adjectives that, when you put them on paper, still feel generic.

  • "Successful."
  • "Sexy."
  • "Adventurous."

Benefits-driven adjectives like these don't feel specific to our experience. and therefore we don't feel them.

Show, don't tell with your writing.

Remember that?

Here's where showing, not telling, becomes the deal maker—and breaker. Because to understand what we're selling on the third, final, and most important psychological Six Appeal level? We need to slice open our readers' minds and spill their thoughts onto the page in their natural form—not through words (like adjectives) that represent their thoughts (telling), but via their actual thoughts (showing). Which, on all accounts, are thoughts that, once we read them, we will feel ourselves. And that's precisely the idea.

So. let's say I'm the show off how the X6 test drives. *wide grin*

One of my personal world views is that hard work trumps luck. This is evidenced by the way I run my business, and how I show up in the world. In fact, most entrepreneurs tend to maintain this mindset. We don't care much for fate, and we believe our individual efforts will pay off. It's why we obsessively chase after every new shiny object—because we actually believe we can have it.

So taking that into account, if I were test driving an X6, here's what I might be thinking:

For all the people from my hometown who called me trailer trash behind my back.
Who's laughing now?
I knew all my hard work would pay off eventually.
You really do reap what you sow.
I might not have the perfect body, but at least I've got this.
If my mom could onlyh see me now.
She would have died over this car.
Okay, that was a bad joke.

Can you see my worldview reflected in those thoughts? I clearly believe that hard work trumps luck, and that circumstances are within our control. I don't believe that a higher power is responsible for my happenstance; I believe that I'm in control of my fate. And as a result, these might be some of the things I'm thinking about when debating whether or not to buy an X6.

However, what if my worldview were slightly different? Perhaps I strongly believed in fate. In destiny. In karma. And perhaps I put more value on God's plan, say, than my plan? How might that change what I'm thinking while I'm test driving an X6, debating whether or not to buy it?

Well, perhaps when test driving the X6, the dialogue in my head goes a little more like:

Lucky me!
I'm so grateful for the apportunities I've been given.
Karma is a beautiful thing.
I knew being a good person would pay off eventually.
Can you see the difference in worldview coming out in these statemens? The first series of statements was centered on a worldview that we, as individuals, have ultimate control over our destinies-and if I'm able to purchase this X6, it's because I worked hard to achieve the right. And the second was centered on a worldview that believed that fate is in control, and if I'm able to purchase this X6, it's because karma did me good, and I've been lucky in life.

Now, naturally, this is just one example using one type of worldview as the angle. There are many world views, and you'll have to select the ones that are going to best help you imagine the perception that your target reader is going to have as it relates to what you're selling.

The worldview on individual effort versus fate applies here because it's going to drive my decision-making process on whether I actually need an X6, or can happily settle for a Subaru.

Similarly, if you're selling a DIY wreath-making kit, you'd have to consider the different world views of the folks most likely to buy this—your target—and tease out the world views that will make a difference in their decision-making process.

Perhaps a relevant world view to consider here is the target's beliefs on what the definition of a successful woman looks like. Does a successful woman look like one who goes out and builds a high-powered career so she can provide for her family? Or does a successful woman look like one who stays home and focuses on being with her family? You can guess the worldview you're going to want to support here. And in turn, those world views are going to influence the thoughts you're imagining running through their heads when they're contemplating buying your DIY wreath-making kit—and furthermore, what we're going to SAY to them, then, to support those thoughts.

That's where we're going next.

We're going to learn how to take your target readers' thoughts and use them to form the structure of your messaging. And we need that messaging to line up with the world views and belief system of your ideal customer. This is about the art of positioning; you can't get in the right position if you don't know where you stand in relation to it.

Ultimately, this all comes down to shared human experience.

When we read something, we're secretly reading to find a piece of ourselves in the stories of others.

We're looking hard to validate our own existence, that the things we feel, we think, we imagine.

We're looking to feel like they aren't just things we experience in isolation, but together as humans-especially the things that aren't talked about very often.

It's why the blog pst you read that reveals that raw truth about being an alcoholic is so seductive.

Not because we're all a bunch of alcoholics, but because we all have our own demons we battle with, and on some level, we've all felt the same pain. Reading that reinforces our own identity and makes us feel okay about being who we are. It makes us feel okay about our past decisions. And it makes us feel okay about being us.

So as a result, we SHARE that piece of writing on the internet, in an effort to show the world who we are, and what we represent. We feel a special bond with the writer, a person we've never met beyond their words on a page. And we develop an emotional connection with their writing---and them. Because we developed an emotional connection with their experience.

A person's thoughts shape their experience.

And so these are the thoughts we must use to guide our writing. Because ultimately, the words you choose to say to them need to reflect their experience back to them, and position yourself as a logical piece among it that seamlessly fits in.

Because if you're selling a person on themselves, then what you're really selling them on?

Is their own thoughts.
