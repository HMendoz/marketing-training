The worldview of your customer

A worldview includes themes, values, emotions, and ethics, part of which comes from your culture, part from your upbringing, and part from your past experiences. It's an all-encompassing perspective on everything that exists and matters to you

In other words, it's a pretty big deal. And it's way more important when it comes to selling than you ever thought.

Your worldview shapes and informs EVERY SINGLE EXPERIENCE YOU'VE EVER HAD. (And ever will have.) You know that whole rose-colored glasses saying? Alright, now imagine them being hot chartreuse glasses, and now imagine you wearing them all the time, day and night. The glasses affect what you see, and how you see it. (Is your mailman hot...chartreuse?) And depending on the degree of transparency of the lens, you might see some things more easily, while other things are de-emphasized, distorted, or in some cases, not even visible at all.

Our behavior is driven by our deepest desires, values and motivations - all of which are directed by our worldviews. And that includes our buying behavior.

And that's why worldview is so imperative for you to understand—much more than demographics or basic psychographics or superficial customer avatars.

Because you have no choice but to adjust the frame of your message depending on which color glasses your buyer is wearing.

Now here's the tricky thing about worldview: A person's beliefs can't, by definition, be proven in the logical sense, because they're considered an axiom. That's to say, the starting point for anyone's reasoning is already biased because it's coming from within their own worldview - and therefore their beliefs are typically argued from rather than argued for. (Read that sentence twice. It's a doozy.)

Most people are pretty oblivious to the specific worldviews they've absorbed, (who knows what growing up in Scranton did to me), but that doesn't mean they're any less relevant. Just because your customer doesn't recognize their own worldviews does not mean they don't exist.

Your customer's worldview underlines every belief, value, and every action they'll take until the day they die. That's why many people live or think in a certain way, without knowing or even considering the worldview from which their belief or behavior derives.

That said, the idea isn't for us to try and explicitly point out someone's worldview with our words. Rather, what we need to do is sit down and take a thorough inventory of what color glasses our buyer might be wearing, and what kinds of beliefs and values might be held by wearing those particular glasses. And then, furthermore, how those beliefs might impact how they view what we're selling—and, ultimately, what we need to say to align our product with their worldview..

If we can put the same colored glasses on, then we don't only better understand what they're seeing, but we can write the words they need to hear - not just any old ones that come to mind. (Which is usually the strategy, right? “They'll get the point,” you think to yourself. But getting the point is not the point. Making them want you is.)

Essentially, The Six Appeal Process is a "pattern-matching" process. And our main goal is to align the pattern of your words with the pattern of their worldview in creative, delightfully surprising new ways that stop them dead in their tracks, and makes them never have to think twice about saying YES.
