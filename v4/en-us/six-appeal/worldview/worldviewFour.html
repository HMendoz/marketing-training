Worldview and worlds

When I did my graduate work in Applied Linguistics, I studied the intersection of language and worldview extensively, and to what extent one affected the other.

Allow me to get academic on you for a moment. (This is when I put on my funky black glasses.) There's a theory in linguistics that posits that the grammatical structure of your native language, (or, how you arrange words into sentences), will forever affect your worldview, and furthermore, how you perceive everything that ever happens to you, from here forevermore. The theory is called Linguistic Relativity, and it encompasses the idea that the language you speak largely influences your thoughts. Furthermore, there's also Linguistic Determinism, which says your language DETERMINES your thought entirely. It's akin to imagining your language as a prison, with your brain held as prisoner. In other words, your thoughts, ideas and worldview are limited by what your language allows you to comprehend.

For example, the language you speak, and the way you use past, present, and future tenses will forever affect how you perceive time and therefore how you perceive the rest of the world. Freaky, huh?

An even more interesting example is writer Ayn Rand, who, in one of her books, made up a world in which the word “I” didn't exist. Can you imagine if you could never talk about yourself and say the word, “I”? What would that mean for your reality? What would that mean if you grew up never having that word in your vocabulary at all?

So how does all of that relate?

Those who speak the online language, and those who don't.

Humans adapt, and quickly. And we have. In this sense, our worldview hasn't been limited by our language. Rather, our worldview is continually changed, challenged and adapted, thanks to technology. That said, if anything, our language has been limited by our worldview.

Who you are determines how you speak, what type of words you use, how you use them, and in which contexts. And for us, this is incredibly relevant as we attempt to bridge the gap between who your customers are, what language they speak, what type of words they use, how they use them, and in which contexts—so we can position you as the only person who really GETS THEM.

This is a worthwhile goal. Humans tribe together, and you need to give them the right cues and signals to help them understand which tribe you (and what you're selling) represent.

No matter what you're selling, if it helps them feel more like them, and helps them feel like they belong in the tribe they've self-identified with, they will want it.

And in this way, our goal is to use language not just for the sake of communicating, but as a powerful creative selling tool to help us reach our goals. We can bend language to our will, and use it to influence thought.

That said, trying to change a person's worldview is a fool's task.

This is also why I advise students and clients to stop trying to convince and persuade people who don't share a similar worldview to buy their stuff.

You know the people you're trying so hard to convince that yoga is the answer to everything? It's an idiot's errand. You're wasting your time. Rather, what we want to do is align ourselves with folks who already share a similar worldview, and then use language to demonstrate how what we're selling supports that worldview in ways they may not have considered prior.

Begin examining the type of language your customer's existing worldview does support, and then start using it to your advantage and weaving it into your message - instead of constantly going against the grain and trying to show them a different way.

Some insight on this from the one and only Seth Godin:

“The worldview of a 45 year old wine-loving investment banker is very different from that of a fraternity brother. One might see a $100 bottle of burgundy as both a bargain and a must-have, while the other might see the very same bottle of wine as an insane waste of money.

Worldview changes three things: attention, bias and vernacular. Attention, because we choose to pay attention to those things that we've decided matter. Bias, because our worldview alters the way we filter and interpret what we hear. And vernacular, because words and images resonate with people differently based on their worldview.”

It's extremely expensive, time consuming and difficult to change someone's worldview. The guys at Opus One shouldn't spend a lot of time marketing expensive wine to fraternities because it's not efficient. Sell nuts to squirrels, don't try to persuade dolphins that nuts are delicious.

So how do you predict someone's worldview in order to align your language with it? (The hard part, right?)

And to that point: Let's first consider yours.

Because your worldview is likely inextricably linked to whatever you're selling, and why you're selling it, and therefore, it follows that those reasons will also influence why the person who's going to buy it, will buy it.

Because in every way, shape, and form, what you're selling is a manifestation of your worldview.
