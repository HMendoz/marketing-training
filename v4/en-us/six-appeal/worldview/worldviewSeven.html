Using worldview to find each other

So this worldview stuff is important. (By which i mean no skipping, fast-forwarding, glossing over, or ignoring.)

It's the foundation of your customer's every belief, and in turn, how they'll feel about what you say to them, and in which ways.

And with that comes this truth:

You cannot successfully sell anything, when, in the process, you accidentally violate someone's sense of IDENTITY.

You've got to support their identity.

Studies show that people will actually avoid buying something that conflicts with their identity. And that makes sense when you think about it, right?

Surprise! I didn't. She naturally assumed I was buying Fiji water as a commodity, and that all water was the same, with a mere difference in price. Yet, I naturally assumed she knew the real reason I was buying the Fiji water: Because it makes a statement about who we are, and what's important to us. I buy Fiji water for the simple reason that the packaging makes me feel gorgeous, as if it were an extension of my own personal style, and that, for me, makes the experience of drinking the water more enjoyable. And that has nothing to do with the water itself, and everything to do with how the damn water makes me feel.

The stupid package is an extension of my personal style, which says something about me: Personal style must be important. And if personal style is important, what might that say about my worldview? Well, a few things:

  1. I value individuality.
  2. Appearance isn't superficial, but animportant extension of who I am.
  3. How I look is who I am.

And if that's the case, then it means that the person in charge of advertising Fiji water, or writing copy for the Fiji label, shouldn't write something about how good the water tastes. (A feature.) They shouldn't write something about the quality of the water. (Again, a feature.) Nor should they mention what a good bargain it is, or how it's a “good deal.” Rather, they need to write something that's going to help me view their water as an extension of me and my worldview.

Therefore, instead of writing something like:

Fiji Water
The world's cleanest, most refreshing H2O.

I might write something like:

Fiji Water
NEVER HIDE.

And this is what we mean by an emotional HOOK statement. We're writing our emotional hook statements to serve as the foundation of our message - to grab the heart strings of the people we want to attract. Only then, once we've got a strong emotional hook statement, do we begin to flesh out the rest of our message-the details, the must-knows, the extras.

We all want brands to inspire us and express our values so well that being a customer becomes a source of pride and joy for us.

Once you've got a hypotesis about your customer's worldview, and hoe it intersects with what you sell, you work to craft a hook statement that will appeal to that worldview-

-even if you never say a word about your product.

You certainly don't have to go that route, though—you can talk about your product. You just need to make sure you're doing it in a way that aligns with the right worldview you're going for.

Imagine I'm a wedding photographer

I don't want to appeal to every bride on the planet, because first of all, it's impossible, and secondly, you certainly must have personal preferences, right? Your personal preferences likely line up with your own worldview, and hence, the worldview of your business. So we want to make sure we get a match.

So let's say I'm this wedding photographer, and I don't want to appeal to the traditional bride who's dreamed of this day since she was a little girl. Maybe I like working with brides who are less traditional. Maybe even skeptical of the whole wedding thing. Maybe they're more fun that way—they allow me to get creative with my photography, and these are the weddings I enjoy most.

So first thing's first: I consider my customer's worldview.

Her worldview is likely that she isn't the “marrying type” and is skeptical of the whole ordeal—but let's say her mother-in-law wanted her first-born son to have a “traditional wedding”...so here the bride is, gagging herself through it.

Now, that's a considerably different worldview from the girl who's been dreaming about the perfect dress since she was ten. And as such, we need to adjust our hook statements to really speak to the right person we want to attract. So instead of saying something expected, (like you'd see in your average movie trailer—which is why I had you do that exercise, so you could first consider what's expected), instead, you might write something like:

The kiss could be awkward.
Your girls might fall out.
There's always a chance of rain.
And nothing's stopping Uncle Harry from dirty dancing with your college roommate.

Good thing we're pros at this.

Do you see how statements like this are FAR more powerful in regards to appealing to the person you want to be working with, rather than some generic blanket statement about once-in-a-lifetime memories and the big walk down the aisle?

Allow yourself to really get in the head of the people you're selling to, and use their thoughts to guide your language. (More on this to come.)

I promise this will get easier as you learn to adopt the right mindset when you write to sell.

For now, don't worry so much about picking the perfect word; worry more about picking the perfect message.

The perfect words will come later.

Eighty percent of the writing process is editing, and therefore brilliant writing isn't a writing process at all.

It's an editing process.

And we're going to edit our way to sales, one step at a time.
